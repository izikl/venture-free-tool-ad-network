What is BMI?

Body Mass Index, usually called BMI, is a quick screening tool that compares your weight with your height. It does not diagnose health conditions on its own, but it gives a useful starting point for understanding whether your body weight falls into a common population range. Doctors, fitness professionals, insurance providers, and public health researchers often use BMI because it is simple, fast, and easy to compare across many people.

BMI categories are widely grouped as follows: underweight is below 18.5, normal weight is 18.5 to 24.9, overweight is 25 to 29.9, and obese is 30 or higher. These ranges help describe general weight status, but they are only one data point. A person can have a normal BMI and still have health concerns, or a high BMI and still be physically fit.

How to calculate BMI

The BMI formula is weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared. For example, if a person weighs 70 kilograms and is 1.75 meters tall, their BMI is 70 divided by 1.75 squared, which is about 22.9. If you use imperial units, convert pounds to kilograms and feet or inches to meters first. This calculator handles those conversions automatically so you can switch between metric and imperial without doing any manual math.

When you calculate BMI, try to enter accurate measurements instead of rounded guesses. Small changes in height, especially, can affect the result because height is squared in the formula. For the most useful reading, measure without shoes and use a recent body weight.

BMI limitations

BMI is helpful, but it has limits. It does not account for muscle mass, bone density, body fat distribution, age-related changes, or athletic build. A muscular athlete may have a high BMI but low body fat. An older adult may have a normal BMI while carrying less muscle and more body fat. BMI also does not show where body fat is stored, which matters because abdominal fat can raise health risk.

Use BMI as a screening estimate, not as the only measure of health. Waist circumference, body composition, blood pressure, lab results, activity level, sleep, and nutrition habits all add important context. If you have concerns about your weight or health risk, a qualified medical professional can interpret BMI alongside the rest of your health profile.

Frequently asked questions

Is BMI accurate for athletes?

Not always. Athletes and people with high muscle mass can have a higher BMI even when they have a healthy body fat level.

Can children use this BMI calculator?

This page is best for adults. BMI interpretation for children and teens uses age and sex specific growth charts.

What BMI is considered healthy?

For most adults, a BMI from 18.5 to 24.9 is considered the normal range. Health status still depends on more than one number.

Should I worry if my BMI is high?

A higher BMI can suggest increased health risk, but it should be reviewed with other measures before drawing conclusions.
